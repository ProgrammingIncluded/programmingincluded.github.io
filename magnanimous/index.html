Where have the righteous magnanimous leaders gone?

21st February 2021 at 12:14am
holiness Ravi Zacharias

Context

A few weeks ago, I received email update on the investigative report of Zacharias' life, I was shocked, disgusted, and ultimately saddened. After various conversations with my friends, I hope that this article captures my thoughts and ultimately points to Scripture as the reason for how I should respond.

My fellow readers, before I begin, I write these words in focusing on the general Christian living. I do not want to burden anyone more than what God is already working on in their life. Christian living is a process and so I trust the Lord in guiding His sheep.

Our heavenly Father is gentle in ways that are unseen and ways I cannot fathom. I bear no personal examples or thoughts about certain persons when writing this article except those pointed out here. Given the weight of the topic, I am firm in delivering some truths here. As always, I am open to discussion feel free to PM at any time. I write these words out of a desire to love to strengthen my brothers and sisters. I firmly believe that truth in a Christian is far more important than to uphold the "renown" of Christianity.

If this is the first time you have been exposed to Christian literature and writing, welcome! Let me know if you have any questions and I would be glad to clarify.

The Pursuit of Holiness

This is the message we have heard from him and proclaim to you, that God is light, and in him there is no darkness at all. If we say we have fellowship with him while we walk in darkness, we lie and do not practice truth.

1 John 1:5-6

Zacharias' misdeed is a grave reminder of the importance of holiness in my walk with the Lord. Great damage and hurt is done when a Christian ignores the pursuit of holiness. Tozer once wrote:

There is an evil which I have seen under the sun and which in its effect upon the Christian religion may be more destructive than communism, Romanism and liberalism combined. It is the glaring disparity between theology and practice among professing Christians.

So wide is the gulf that separates theory from practice in the Church that an inquiring stranger who chances upon both would scarcely dream that there was any relation between them. An intelligent observer of our human scene who heard the Sunday morning sermon and later watched the Sunday afternoon conduct of those who had heard it would conclude that he had been examining two distinct and contrary religions.

A church conference, for instance, may listen to and applaud the most spiritual message, and twenty minutes later adopt the most carnal procedure, altogether as if they had not heart the impassioned moral appeal in a few moments before. Christians habitually weep and pray over beautiful truth, only to draw back from that same truth when it comes to the difficult job of putting it in practice. The average church simply does not dare to check its practices against biblical precepts. It tolerates things that are diametrically opposed to the will of God, and if the matter is pointed out to its leaders they will defend its unscriptural practices with a smooth casuistry equal to the verbal dodging of the Roman moralists.

Root of Righteousness Chapter 14 by A. W. Tozer

Tozer writes with sharp observation, the constant struggle that invades the church today. I have heard many defenses when one accuses a Christian of hypocrisy. The Christian often defends by noting the falsity of the accusation or by victimizing themselves as being against an increasingly secularized world. Though some of these defenses may be true, Tozer's observation is a stark reminder of a Christian's constant struggle with the pursuit of holiness.

Inevitably, a Christian must be willing to accept, in brutal honesty, the disparity of the lives we live and the holiness acceptable to our Lord. It should be in our every effort to reduce this disparity. When accused of our failures we should first pray earnestly that the Lord has sent a lesson for us to learn and to turn but also the wisdom to know how to respond. We each have blind spots. Such accusations ought to humble us to excel further still.

Insomuch as there is beauty in the doctrine of justification by faith, no where in Scripture does it show us the negligence of the works that ought to be produced from faith in our lives. In fact, quite the contrary.

The Book of 1 John

The pursuit of holiness is one of the core themes of 1 John. The book focuses on this push and pull between walking in righteousness and repentance of our sins:

But if we walk in the light, as he is in the light, we have fellowship with one another, and the blood of Jesus his Son cleanses us from all sin. If we say we have no sin, we deceive ourselves, and the truth is not in us. If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just to forgive us our sins and to cleanse us from all unrighteousness. If we say we have not sinned, we make him a liar, and his word is not in us.

1 John 1:7-10

Then later onwards:

My little children, I am writing these things to you so that you may not sin. But if anyone does sin, we have an advocate with the Father, Jesus Christ the righteous. He is the propitiation for our sins, and not for ours only but also for the sins of the whole world. And by this we know that we have come to know him, if we keep his commandments. Whoever says “I know him” but does not keep his commandments is a liar, and the truth is not in him, but whoever keeps his word, in him truly the love of God is perfected. By this we may know that we are in him: whoever says he abides in him ought to walk in the same way in which he walked.

1 John 2:1-6

Non-exhaustively we can see this again in 1 John 3:1-10 and 1 John 5:1-5.

How About Other Books?

Other books I can think of is James, the proverbs of the New Testament. Not even just the New Testament but also the Old Testaments, look at the number of kings and God's description of their lives through what they have done and ultimately their hearts. God has not changed from the Old Testament to the New Testament. Paul has written this topic as well in Romans, Corinthians, to name a few.

The Christian reality is that we sin, we fail, we turn sideways, and we have blind spots (Hebrews 3:12-18). But eventually we turn and pursuit that which is good. Turning may take a day, weeks, months, or even years. But nevertheless we turn away from wrong, acknowledge our failures, and continue forward in our race until eventually we are called to cross to the other side where we are finally perfected without faults, wrong is made right, purified, cleaned, and refreshed (Philippians 1:6; Revelation 21)

Why Holiness is Important: An Allegiance

I think 1 John 1:5-6 sums it up nicely: we worship a God of light and naturally we ought to practice what is right. There is much to be said about this matter i.e. fruits of the Spirit. However, without diving into the nitty gritty, one way I think of this is like an "allegiance."

If we proclaim an allegiance to God as our King, should it not be the case that we are called to represent Him? Aren't we called to be "ambassadors" (2 Corinthians 5:20)? If who we follow allows us to scream "hallelujah" on the top of roofs, wouldn't we want to do whatever we can to please the one we follow? If it happens that the God we worship is truth, love, just, set apart and holy, our walk in this life should reflect that.

How great it is that we can pursue holiness without a concern for trying to achieve perfection to enter into heaven. Pursuing holiness is a great way to please our heavenly Father and, furthermore, removes the devastating sting of sin. As Piper once preached:

Holiness is fundamentally an affection issue, not a behavioral issue. This is only emphasized by the fact that all the Law and the Prophets — all the prohibitions and warnings pertaining to our behaviors, the height of holiness — are summed up in the greatest commandments to love God with all we are and our neighbors as ourselves (Matthew 22:37–40). Holiness looks most like the delight of true love. And if we love Jesus, we will keep his commandments — meaning that when our affections are really engaged, our behaviors naturally follow (John 14:15).

"Holiness will make you unbelievably happy" John Piper

Self-Assessment

It is at this point that I want to remind myself and my readers that holiness is something that we ultimately have to focus on and we ourselves should hold to a higher scrutiny first than to others. There are things to be said about how to point out sin for others but that is not my focus in this discussion.

In my discussion of holiness, I am urging for a healthy dose of self-assessment. Wisdom, discernment, and many other factors have to be considered when talking about building up others in love and urging others to holiness, delivering truth in a silver plater, getting the log out of your own eye first, etc. (Ephesians 5 amongst many others.) I will not be covering that today in this discussion.

That Terrible Thing

When I first read Zacharias' report, I was shocked and disgusted. It took me a few minutes to recompose and think more about how I ought to respond.

Zacharias has introduced many different thinkers and resources in my life, as I am sure, for many others. It is with great sadness that I come to acknowledge the misconducts. That being said, I want to face this with the right perspective. Around the week of his death, I wrote an article on Zacharias (Ravi Zacharias, On Christian Knowledge, and the Ought to Know), I think it is only right to mention him once again in light of new information. Here are my thoughts.

Appreciated RZIM's Response

RZIM (Ravi Zacharias International Ministries) had posted an article on why they think it was necessary to expose and publicize Zacharias' misdeeds: https://www.rzim.org/read/rzim-updates/theological-statement

I appreciate their desire to honor the Lord through this. They outline their reasoning in addressing the issue as follows:

  • The Glory of the God
    • 1 Corinthians 10:31
  • The Demands and Consequences of Truth
    • 1 John 1:5-6
    • Ephesians 5:11, 13
  • The Demands and Consequences of Stewardship
    • 1 Corinthians 4:1-2
  • The Demand for Repentance and Restoration
    • 2 Corinthians 7:10

I am sure this was not an easy decision to make. Ultimately since this issue was publicly exposed by those involved before the investigation, I am inclined to think this allegation to be on a public level and should be addressed as such.

Compared that to how Hollywood and many other known predators in the world often get away with things, RZIM's stance to expose and acknowledge these problems is the loving and right thing to do.

I hope through all this, the victims can be restored and the Gospel ultimately to shine through. It is through the Gospel that Zacharias' evil deeds can ultimately made right. It is only in Christ that we can reconcile what has happened.

Interpretation of the Misconduct in Hindsight

I see this in hindsight, it is the judgement of God to expose Zacharias' sin publicly. I think about David when I say this. God's judgement on David's sin with Bathsheba (both sexual sins and murder), which the consequences were (as spoken by the Prophet Nathan 2 Samuel 12:7-15):

  • Great Turmoil in David's House (v10)
    • Ammon and Tamar (2 Samuel 13)
    • Absalom's Conspiracy (2 Samuel 14-20)
  • The Public Defilement of David's concubines (v11; 2 Samuel 16:20-23)
  • Death of David's child (v14; 2 Samuel 12:15-23)

I used to read David's story and think how terrifying the judgement was, perhaps it seems a bit unfair. But yet, the more I think about the impact of sexual sin, the broken families, and the difficulties that people go through, I think the consequences of the judgement is perhaps more fair than it first seems. Though I wish no one to ever have to go through that, I believe the Lord has perfectly good and wise reasons for this judgement.

The judgement of David's actions must be viewed in light of the fact that David was king, he committed both murder and sexual sins. David was to lead Israel and the acts of the king influences Israel (see history in 1 & 2 Kings). One can pretty much say he set an example for the people around him as elders do in the church. It is no wonder there are high standards for kings, for example:

It is an abomination for kings to commit wicked acts, For a throne is established on righteousness.

Proverbs 16:12

In our day and age, modern culture makes death seem very devastating, for an atheistic worldview, death is the ceasing of self. But we must remember death is a transition of this life to another. David remarks "I am going to him, but he will not return to me." 2 Samuel 12:23 on the passing of his infant son. All humans will dwell forever in eternity, it is a matter of where.

What reminded me of David in Zacharias' sin is the public exposure of his sexual sin. However, outside of that, the similarities end. Zacharias' misconduct and consequence was different than that of David's, and I do not ultimately know what God has allowed and prevented in terms of consequences and judgement of Zacharias' actions. Furthermore, in the case of Zacharias, these issues were brought up after his death, so we are unable to see how he would have responded.

David ultimately repented and turned towards the Lord and was made right with God through faith (Psalm 51; Hebrews 11:32). I do not know what would've happened to Zacharias given the reports had he received them while being alive, but all I can do is trust in the Lord's fairness and pray for those affected.

What to Do About Zacharias' Works

With a broken trust, ultimately it undermines the credibility of Zacharias' academic works in Christendom and also his legacy. Thankfully Zacharias' works do not appear in a vacuum. However, more importantly, we have a surer foundation, which is the word of the Lord. I think the arguments of his work stand apart from his credibility and ought to be analyzed in academia independently but only time will tell. Once again I can only trust the Lord's decision as time progresses.

A Grave Reminder

I am grateful that we live in a society where sexual sins like rape can be publicly called out and hated by the general public. We live in a several centuries old culture influenced by Judeo-Christian thinking where such acts are publicly condemnable. That is not always the case depending on the culture even if said culture have felt the damage sexual sins have caused. There are many who desire to speak out but cannot.

As much as I am grateful that sexual sins are publicly condemned in U.S., however, so often I think the condemnation is done with a "self-righteous" attitude. A "us vs. them" mentality: "I would never conduct acts of a predator if I was in their position." I do wonder about that "self-righteous attitude" and how truly we can stand the test of power.

Imagine a life where you suddenly had the ability to go anywhere in the world with a jet, say a word and a things get done, and everyone looked up to you. How would one operate? I think most of us would have struggled in one way or another. With all this power, our secret struggles that we have yet to fight-off will only be strengthened. This is why we hear so many issues with sexual sins in those of leadership (MLK, Tiger Woods, Weinstein, etc.) Even those without malicious intent, fall victim to their own selves. It is no wonder it is hard to be a leader.

Jesus said:

“You have heard that it was said, ‘You shall not commit adultery.’ But I say to you that everyone who looks at a woman with lustful intent has already committed adultery with her in his heart. If your right eye causes you to sin, tear it out and throw it away. For it is better that you lose one of your members than that your whole body be thrown into hell. And if your right hand causes you to sin, cut it off and throw it away. For it is better that you lose one of your members than that your whole body go into hell.

Matthew 5:27-30

Jesus outlines the importance of the heart, by lusting after someone, we have already committed adultery. All actions must first stem from our intents. Once our heart intents exist, it is a matter of how far that path we walk in committing adultery, not a question of if we will. Knowing my own heart, I should have a healthy dose of concern about the actions that would stem from myself if I was to suddenly gain leadership roles and responsibilities. What would I do? What would I say? How would I be kept accountable?

Soon after my initial shock, the report brought to me a solemn reminder of this reality. I am not vouching for fear of sin or joylessness, but rather an embrace of the Gospel and our fear of the Lord. Ephesians 5 captures this well:

Therefore be imitators of God, as beloved children. And walk in love, as Christ loved us and gave himself up for us, a fragrant offering and sacrifice to God. But sexual immorality and all impurity or covetousness must not even be named among you, as is proper among saints. Let there be no filthiness nor foolish talk nor crude joking, which are out of place, but instead let there be thanksgiving.

Ephesians 5:1-4

We have been given much in Christ and we ought to celebrate with thanksgiving! But at the same time, we mustn't forget where we once were and the constant battles against ourselves.

Run the Race Strong

This is why I brought up the pursuit of holiness. Every day we are tasked to schedule our life. As each hour passes by, how are we living the time allocated to us? This is a hard thing but I urge us to fight for our disciplines and to take control of our lives for the Lord. Do not wait for a trial to become our forcing function or wait until marriage or [insert major life event here]. Start with the simple stuff, start now. We have each been called to be in a career and in our immediate family. I am sure holiness will directly impact these spheres of influence for the better.

Summary

In conclusion, I am heart broken by the fact of the misconducts I pray for the victims involved that they can be restored rightly. The news is a reminder of a pursuit of my own personal holiness. I pray for my brothers and sisters in Christ to do what is righteous and continue to seek ways to please the Lord in their private lives as well as publicly.
